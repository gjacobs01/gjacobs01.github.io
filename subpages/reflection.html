ENC2135 Reflection

During my time taking ENC2135, there have been several projects that have tested my skills as a writer, yet at the same time taught me what it means to be a good writer. My topic of choice, AI’s role in software development, covered a wide variety of contexts, ranging from formal academic papers to entertaining yet informative blog posts and infographics. Before I took this course, I never thought of anything beyond the content of my writing, nor did I write anything with the same scale of this semester’s projects. At first, it was rather intimidating, but having a topic related to a field I am passionate about made it more approachable. This also allowed me to focus on how I could improve my writing beyond content and analysis. I’ve realized that top notch writing involves thoughtful choices that dictate what sources should be used, how ideas are structured, and how to design the work with an audience or goal in mind.

The information and sources you decide to use are arguably the most important part of writing. When done improperly, it can set you up for failure before you even begin writing. Our first project involved finding sources and introduced the idea of the peer review process. I’ve always assumed that all academic sources were created equal, but after learning what peer review means, it’s made me consider what sources to use more carefully. I learned that there are some great sites like Research Gate and arXiv that have ways of finding peer reviewed sources, which will likely be useful in the future. The first project also taught me how to write citations in a citation style relevant to the field I am studying. These concepts are important when writing academically, so learning them early on will make a big impact on my future academic career.

Like the process of choosing sources, building credibility and the process of appealing to a specific audience is another important skill I learned. Our project 2 focused on this idea and taught me how to communicate the same message to different audiences. Applying different writings styles and word usages can create a much more enjoyable experience for the audience you are writing for. Appealing to an audience doesn’t just include word choice and tone, it oftentimes starts with choosing the right genre. Genre in the context of writing mediums isn’t something I’ve heard of nor practiced, yet once I had time to develop some genres of my own, I began to understand it’s importance. I saw this happen when I had to choose which artifact to revise from project 2. The first draft of my infographic felt like it was trying to squeeze something like my blog post into an infographic. I instead used a very common “do’s and don’ts” design for my second revision, which is a classic in the infographic genre. Having the right genre for the right audience can make the presentation feel more seamless and less awkward.

The process of revising the infographic also included an important takeaway from this course, that being structure. By the time I had finished project two, I had realized that my artifacts and the field essay contained the same information yet were all different. This led me to conclude that form equals function. Structure isn’t chosen by happenstance; it’s chosen in a way that gets the reader to think a certain way. My field essay for example had a background history section about the development of computer science technology. Not because I wanted to give my readers a good time learning about the exciting history of programming languages, but because the section set up one of the main ideas of the paper. The paper was then organized by relevant key points that grouped several sources together and analyzed them. My executive summary on the other hand had no fluff, background knowledge, or analysis. Only a short and succinct summary of AI programming technology that could be read in minutes. It delivered conclusions and straightforward solutions due to the need for executives to make quick split-second decisions without being bogged down by pages of technical documents. This sort of relationship between form and function makes future decisions regarding the way I communicate easier to make. It requires me to think about what the person I’m communicating with actually wants rather than picking a form of communication that I’m comfortable with.

The projects I worked on over the last semester have certainly given me a great opportunity to learn and grow as a writer. I’m glad that I have improved on the areas I sought to improve at the beginning of the semester, and I feel like I’m prepared for the next few years of writing I have waiting for me. However, one of the most unexpected yet exciting skills I’ve learned in this class has been vicariously lived out through this project. When I heard that project 3 involved creating a website for an e-portfolio, I felt obliged as a computer science major to take shot at building one from scratch. I’ve had a lot of fun, and I’ve learned a lot doing this final project. Allowing us to do our projects on topics we’re passionate about was good enough, but the opportunity to get into web development was the cherry on top. I went into this project without knowing anything about html, CSS, and java, and by the end I made a nice website to show it. By using GitHub’s native web hosting features, I was able to create a website for free that, in turn, will serve as the starting point for a professional e-portfolio for my software development career. Practically speaking, I gained a lot from having an opportunity like this, and while I could have made a webpage using a pre-built web builder in less time, nothing beats having something that you can call your own. Overall, I’m satisfied with what I learned and how my projects turned out, and I hope to have more opportunities like this.
